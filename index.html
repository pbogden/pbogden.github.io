Taking on the data deluge

The U.S. Government's open data initiatives create all sorts of opportunities. The challenge is to extract useful information from the data deluge. Recent advances in data visualization can help, as evidenced by some of the stunning graphics at the New York Times. For example...

Demo

If you're using a modern browser, you'll see two plots below of the earthquakes that occurred in the last 7 days. The Google Map shows their locations, and the scatter plot shows when they occured. These kinds of visualizations aren't new, and USGS has allowed you to download their data for years. What's relatively new is that your browser automatically loaded the real-time data from USGS when you opened this web page. The dynamic transaction took no effort on your part. That's just the beginning...

Bummer! Your seem to be using an old browser...
This demo only works in modern browsers that support JavaScript.

You also downloaded some software for analyzing your newly acquired data. Try it. First, click and drag your mouse on the lower plot to create a "filter" for the data displayed on the map. (Use your finger if you're using a tablet or smart phone.) After you create the filter -- a translucent gray box on the lower plot -- you can drag it along the lower plot to filter (dynamically) the data displayed on the map. Once you get the hang of it, you can use the filter to drill down into the data and geolocate the largest earthquakes, or the smallest, or the most recent, etc. Your browser has become a simple and powerful tool that blurs the distinction between analysis and visualization.

Spreadsheets beware.

This only begins to demonstrate the potential for new kinds of data-driven applications. If you're a pivot table fan, then think about how long it would take you to do the same thing with your favorite spreadsheet program or GIS, if you can do it at all. This isn't a new software product. It's a new approach to data analytics and visualization.
