Open Data, Open Standards & Open Source

The "open triad" can turn a browser into a powerful tool for analytics and visualization. You may be familiar with modern, data-driven documents already. The New York Times has them all the time. These amazing visualizations demonstrate two parts of the triad: "open standards" and "open source" software. "Open data" completes the triad, creating new opportunities for "plucking the diamond from the waste" of the data deluge. (Reference: The Economist.) Time to pluck a diamond...

Demo

The plots below provide two perspectives on the earthquakes that occurred in the last 7 days. The Google Map shows their locations, and the scatter plot underneath shows how their magnitudes evolved over time. These kinds of plots aren't new, and USGS has allowed you to download similar data for years. What's relatively new is that your browser automatically loaded the latest data from USGS and then combined it with the Map it got from Google. Thanks in part to the open triad, this dynamic transaction took no effort on your part. But wait, there's more...

What you may not yet realize is that you also downloaded some software that you can use to analyze your newly acquired data. Try it. (Note: it only works with modern, standards-compliant browsers.) First, click and drag your mouse on the lower plot to create a filter for the data displayed on the map (use your finger if you're viewing this page on a tablet or smart phone). After you create the filter (a translucent gray box on the lower plot), you can drag it along the lower plot to filter (dynamically) the data displayed on the map. Once you get the hang of it, you can use the filter to drill down into the data and geolocate the largest earthquakes, or the smallest, or the most recent, etc. The "open triad" has turned your browser into a simple and powerful tool that blurs the distinction between analysis and visualization. That's just the beginning...

The surface is scratched. Spreadsheets beware.

This only begins to demonstrate the potential for new kinds of browser-based applications. If you're a pivot table fan, then think about how long it would take you to do the same thing with your favorite spreadsheet program or GIS, if you can do it at all. The "open triad" isn't a new spreadsheet application or business-intelligence software product. It's an approach to creating data-driven applications that "pluck diamonds from the waste." If this is sounding like overblown browser hype, then check out what Google is doing with browsers. (Microsoft is watching them, you can bet on it.) Time for a real-world application...

Too much data for a browser? Not a problem.

Okay, browsers do have limitations. So maybe it's time to start talking about the cloud. Potomac Boat Club (PBC) is using the cloud and the open triad to solve their big-data problem. Now PBC can start to offer their members a range of browser-based, data-driven services. Their new app includes real-time safety reports (i.e., who's on the water, how long have they been out, are they overdue?), racing ladders for their super competitive members, resource-utilization reports for their Board of Governors, scheduling tools for their fleet of boats, etc. PBC's wish list for their new app is long and it's growing. So now it's time to start talking about another 21st-century tech-success story: agile software development. Maybe later...
