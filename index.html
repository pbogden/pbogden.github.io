Open Data, Open Standards & Open Source

The "open triad" can turn a browser into a powerful tool for analytics and visualization. You may be familiar with modern, data-driven graphics already. The New York Times has them all the time. These amazing dynamic visualizations demonstrate two parts of the triad: "open-standards" and "open-source" software. "Open data" completes the triad, and creates new opportunities to "pluck the diamond from the waste" of the data deluge. This article in The Economist describes the big data problem. Time to pluck a diamond...

Demo

The plots below provide two perspectives on earthquakes that occurred in the last 7 days. The Google Map shows their locations, and the scatter plot underneath shows how their magnitudes evolved over time. USGS has been providing these kinds of data for years, and these kinds of plots have around for a while. What's relatively new is that your browser automatically loaded the data from USGS and then combined it with the Map it loaded from Google. Thanks in part to "open data" and "open standards", this dynamic transaction took no effort on your part.

What you may not yet realize is that this you also loaded some "open source" software that you can use to start analyzing your newly acquired data in your browser (provided you have a modern, standards-compliant browser). Try it. First, click and drag your mouse on the lower plot to create a filter for the data displayed on the map (use your finger if you're viewing this page on a tablet or smart phone). After you create the filter (a gray box on the lower plot), you can drag it along the lower plot to dynamically filter the data displayed on the map. Once you get the hang of it, you can use the filter to drill down into the data and geolocate the largest earthquakes, or the smallest, or the most recent, etc. The "open triad" has allowed your browser to become an easy-to-use tool that blurs the distinction between analysis and visualization.

The surface is scratched. Spreadsheets beware.

This only begins to demonstrate the potential for new kinds of browser-based applications. If you're a pivot table fan, then think about how long it would take you to do the same thing with your favorite spreadsheet program or GIS, if you could do it at all. The "open triad" isn't a new spreadsheet application or business-intelligence software product. It's an approach to data-driven applications and that provides new opportunities to "pluck the diamond from the waste."

Too much data for a browser? Not a problem.

Okay, browsers have limitations. So maybe it's time to start talking about the cloud. Potomac Boat Club (PBC) is using the cloud to solve their big-data problem and provid their members a range of browser-based services. Their new app includes a variety of data-driven visualization and analytics tools, including real-time safety reports (i.e., who's on the water, how long have they been out, are they overdue?), racing ladders for their super competitive members, resource-utilization reports for their Board of Governors, scheduling tools for their fleet of boats, etc. PBC's wish list for this new data-driven application is long and it's growing. So it's time to start talking about another 21st-century tech-success story: agile software development. Maybe later.
